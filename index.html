During COVID, I worked part time at Code Ninjas , which is an awesome company with a pretty straightforward mission. Teach kids how to write code.

I am frankly quite surprised that programming is not taught much more heavily in school, especially junior high and high schools. This is a good segue to the next paragraph ...

While I was in university, I volunteered for Schulich Ignite, which is an offshoot of Google Ignite.

Google Ignite was an initiative aimed at making programming more accessible for those wanting to learn more. After a few years, the broader program fizzled out, but was survived by the remaining university club now called Schulich Ignite, at the University of Calgary.

My first real solo software project is called Jukebox! You can check it out here.

Based on the "room" concept popularized by Jackbox, friends can simply join together via a room code and queue up songs of their choosing.

I intend on adding more functionality in the future, such as SoundCloud and Apple Music.

It was originally my friend Chris' idea, which I had the pleasure of making into a reality. I even got some help along the way, as he recently switched into software development himself!

Probably the coolest job in the world, is working at School of Rock . the famous movie starring Jack Black is based off of the original school, not the other way around (a common mistake).

School of Rock blends music and education in an awesome way through their unique group lessons, where we teach kids how to both be in a band and subsequently play shows!

Teaching music was also my very first job, where I taught drums and guitar to kids in my community as a young teenager.

I also need to use this space to endorse some of my favourite music education creators: Adam Neely, David Bennett, and 8-bit Music Theory, to name a few.

Ah, the elusive music-software-education holy trinity. Maybe one day, I can teach people about some of the best music software that's out there.

If you have a music reading/writing background, you should 100% check out MuseScore. What they have slowly built over the years is mind-boggling, and it just keeps getting better and better! (and did I mention that it's free???)

Now, if you're not classically trained, but are looking to mess around and make some music, I'd recommend a DAW like Ableton, or for Mac users, maybe GarageBand or ProTools.
